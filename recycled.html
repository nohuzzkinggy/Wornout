From Wornout to Reborn

The lifecycle of a garment doesn't have to end in landfill. Here's how recycled and reworn fashion works — and why it matters.

01

What Does "Recycled Fashion" Actually Mean?

Recycled fashion covers a spectrum of approaches. At one end, you have upcycling — a designer taking a vintage leather jacket and transforming it into something new. At the other, industrial-scale textile recycling: shredding fibres, reprocessing them, and spinning them into new yarn. Between these extremes lies everything from deadstock fabric use to rental models to repair culture.

The term "wornout recyclable fashion" specifically refers to garments that have reached the end of their usable life in their current form but still contain recoverable material value. These aren't thrifted gems — they're the items that are too worn, too faded, or too damaged to resell. And they're the most underserved category in the sustainable fashion conversation.

02

The Technology Behind It

The biggest challenge in textile recycling has always been mixed fibres. A cotton-polyester blend is notoriously difficult to separate. But new chemical recycling technologies are changing this. Companies like Renewlane and Infinited Fiber are developing processes that can dissolve cotton from blended fabrics, leaving the synthetic fibres intact for separate recovery.

Worn Again Technologies has partnered with H&M Group and Kering to develop a solvent-based system capable of separating and recovering both polyester and cotton from mixed-fibre textiles at scale. The resulting materials are chemically identical to virgin equivalents.

Infinited Fiber's process starts with cotton-rich textile waste — including old jeans, T-shirts, and cardboard. Through a chemical process, they produce a new cellulose carbamate fibre with properties similar to modal. H&M, ZARA, and Bestseller have all invested in the technology.
03

Brands Getting It Right

Some brands have built their entire identity around the recycled garment. Patagonia's Worn Wear programme has repaired over 100,000 garments and resold worn items since its launch. Their ReCrafted line creates new products entirely from damaged or unsaleable garments — each piece is one-of-a-kind.

Eileen Fisher's Renew programme takes back any Eileen Fisher garment, regardless of condition. Wearable pieces are cleaned and resold. Unwearable ones are cut, felted, and made into new products. Since 2009, the programme has taken back over 1.4 million garments.

On the mass-market end, Levi's has partnered with third-party recycling programmes. Their denim is notoriously recyclable — the same cotton and indigo fibres can be recovered and respun indefinitely, in theory.

Mechanical Recycling

Fibres are shredded and re-spun mechanically. Cheaper, more established. Results in shorter, weaker fibres that typically can only be used in lower-grade applications like insulation, padding, or industrial wiping cloths.

Established

Chemical Recycling

Fibres are dissolved and regenerated chemically. Preserves fibre length and quality. Can produce material equivalent to virgin. Currently expensive and not yet at full industrial scale — but the future of closed-loop textile recycling.

Emerging
04

Why Wornout ≠ Worthless

There's a cultural shift needed as much as a technological one. We've been trained to see worn clothing as waste. But from a materials perspective, a heavily worn cotton T-shirt contains exactly the same cellulose as a new one — it's just been rearranged by use. The fibres are shorter, yes, but they're still fibres.

Reframing wornout clothing as a raw material input rather than an end-of-life product changes how designers, manufacturers, and consumers relate to it. It changes the economics. It changes the infrastructure we build. And it changes what we're willing to pay for — both the initial garment and its end-of-life processing.
