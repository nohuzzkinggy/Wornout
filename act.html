Change What You Wear

Knowledge is useless without action. Here's what you can actually do — as a consumer, a citizen, and a person who wears clothes.

You don't need to be perfect. You need to be intentional. The goal isn't zero consumption — it's conscious consumption. Every decision you make about clothing carries weight. Here's how to make it count.

As a Consumer

01

Buy Less, Choose Well

The most sustainable garment is the one you don't buy. Before purchasing, ask: Do I have something that already does this job? Will I wear this 30 times? Is this replacing something I'll keep using? Applying a 30-wears rule before any purchase is one of the most effective behaviour changes you can make.

02

Learn Basic Repairs

A dropped hem, a missing button, a small tear — these are not the end of a garment. Learning five basic sewing repairs can extend the life of your wardrobe by years. YouTube tutorials, community sewing classes, and repair cafes (a growing movement in many cities) can get you started.

03

Use Take-Back Schemes

H&M, Zara, Levi's, Patagonia, and many others operate in-store garment collection programmes. Wornout items that you can't donate should go here rather than landfill. These programmes aren't perfect, but they keep fibres in the recycling system. Use them.

04

Shop Secondhand First

Charity shops, vintage stores, Vinted, Depop, ThredUp, and local markets. Before buying new, look for it secondhand. The secondhand market is projected to be worth $350 billion by 2027 — and it's growing fastest among Gen Z consumers.

05

Wash Cold, Line Dry

60% of a garment's lifetime emissions come from how it's washed and dried, not how it's made. Switching to cold washes reduces carbon impact. Line drying instead of tumble drying eliminates drying-related emissions entirely and extends fibre life — heat is the enemy of fabric longevity.

Brands Worth Supporting

These companies have committed to meaningful recycled content targets, take-back programmes, or circular design principles — not just marketing claims.

Patagonia
68% of materials recycled or renewable. Worn Wear repair + resale programme. B Corp certified.
Eileen Fisher
Renew take-back programme active since 2009. Over 1.4M garments reclaimed. Felted and remade on-site.
Girlfriend Collective
Activewear made from recycled plastic bottles and fishing nets. Take-back programme for worn items.
Nudie Jeans
Organic cotton denim, free repairs for life in-store. End-of-life collection turns old denim into new fabric.
Rapanui
Wind-powered production. Closed-loop model: garments return at end of life and are fully respun.
Thought Clothing
Natural and recycled fibres. Transparent supply chain. Annual sustainability reports with real data.

As a Citizen

A

Demand Policy Change

The EU's Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles requires brands to offer take-back schemes by 2025 and sets extended producer responsibility targets. The UK lags behind. Write to your MP, support Fashion Revolution's campaigns, and sign petitions demanding mandatory recycled content minimums.

B

Ask Harder Questions

When you buy, ask: Where was this made? What is it made from? What happens when it wears out? Brands that can't answer these questions clearly don't deserve your money. The pressure to be transparent starts with consumers willing to ask.

C

Share What You Learn

The cultural shift in fashion requires more people to understand the problem. Share this site. Recommend documentaries (The True Cost, RiverBlue, The Machinists). Talk about it. Fashion feels like a personal choice — but at scale, it's a collective one.

This is Not About Guilt

Nobody is asking you to own three garments and wear them until they disintegrate. The fashion system is structurally broken in ways that individual choices alone can't fix. But individual choices, multiplied by millions, become market signals. And market signals become industry change. Start somewhere. Start today.
