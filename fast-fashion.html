The Fast Fashion Problem

How a $100 billion industry built its profits on the assumption that clothing is disposable — and what that assumption is costing the planet.

01

What is Fast Fashion?

Fast fashion is a design, manufacturing, and marketing model that produces high volumes of clothing rapidly in response to the latest trends. Unlike traditional seasonal collections, fast fashion brands release new styles weekly — sometimes daily. The model prioritises speed and low cost above all else.

Brands like Zara, H&M, SHEIN, and Primark have perfected this model. SHEIN, at its peak, was adding between 2,000 and 10,000 new styles per day to its platform. The clothes are cheap enough to wear once and discard without guilt — and that's precisely the point.

Average number of weeks a fast fashion garment is worn before being discarded
02

The Supply Chain Nobody Sees

To make a £5 T-shirt, someone, somewhere is being paid almost nothing. The fast fashion supply chain runs through Bangladesh, Cambodia, Vietnam, and Ethiopia — countries where labour laws are weak and enforcement weaker. Workers in these factories routinely work 14-hour days for wages that don't cover basic living costs.

The 2013 Rana Plaza collapse in Dhaka, Bangladesh, which killed 1,134 garment workers, exposed the human cost of fashion's race to the bottom. A decade later, the structural dynamics that led to that disaster remain largely unchanged.

The Rana Plaza Building housed five garment factories. Workers had reported cracks in the building the day before the collapse. Management told them to return to work anyway. The clothes being made inside were for brands including Primark, Benetton, and Walmart.
03

Where Does It All Go?

The UK alone sends 350,000 tonnes of clothing to landfill every year. Globally, less than 1% of material used to produce clothing is recycled into new clothing. The rest either goes to landfill, incinerators, or is exported to developing nations — where it disrupts local textile industries and often ends up in waterways.

The Kantamanto market in Accra, Ghana, receives 15 million items of second-hand clothing every week. Much of it arrives too worn to sell. It gets dumped in the sea, burned on beaches, or left to pile up in the streets.

🏭 Fashion produces 10% of humanity's carbon emissions — more than aviation and shipping combined
💧 A single pair of jeans requires 7,500 litres of water to produce — the same as one person drinking for 7 years
🌊 500,000 tonnes of plastic microfibers enter the ocean each year from washing synthetic clothes
04

The Psychological Hook

Fast fashion works partly because of how it makes us feel. The dopamine hit of a new purchase, amplified by social media aesthetics and influencer hauls, creates a cycle of consumption that is deliberately difficult to break. Brands invest enormously in making their products feel aspirational while pricing them at impulse-buy levels.

The result is a generation of consumers who own more clothes than any generation in history — and yet feel like they have nothing to wear. The average person in the UK owns 118 items of clothing and wears just 44% of them regularly.
