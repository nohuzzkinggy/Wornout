Why It's Good for the Planet

The environmental case for recycled and wornout fashion is overwhelming. Here's the data — and why it matters.

Choosing recycled garments over new ones isn't a small lifestyle tweak. Done at scale, it represents one of the most meaningful levers we have for reducing fashion's environmental footprint. Here's how the numbers break down.

💧

Water

79 Billion m³

of water used by fashion annually

Recycled cotton uses up to 98% less water than growing virgin cotton. Cotton is one of the world's most water-intensive crops — the Aral Sea, once one of the largest lakes in the world, was largely drained to irrigate Central Asian cotton fields.

Every T-shirt made from recycled fibres saves approximately 2,700 litres of water. At scale, shifting even 20% of cotton production to recycled content would save billions of litres annually.

🌍

Carbon

-70%

carbon reduction from recycled vs virgin fibre

Producing recycled polyester from PET plastic bottles generates up to 70% fewer carbon emissions than virgin polyester from petroleum. For cotton, mechanical recycling still requires significant energy, but chemical recycling processes are approaching 50-60% reductions.

Extending the life of a garment by just nine extra months reduces its carbon, water, and waste footprint by 20-30% each.

♻️

Waste

1%

of clothing currently recycled into new clothing

Of the 92 million tonnes of textile waste generated globally per year, less than 1% is recycled back into clothing. The rest goes to landfill or incineration. Scaling textile-to-textile recycling to just 25% would reduce landfill contributions from fashion by tens of millions of tonnes annually.

Countries with the highest textile recovery rates — like Germany and the Netherlands — show what's possible with the right infrastructure and consumer habits.

🌿

Land & Chemicals

200K+

tonnes of toxic dyes discharged into water annually

Conventional cotton occupies 2.5% of the world's agricultural land while using 16% of all insecticides. Growing less virgin cotton through recycled alternatives directly reduces pesticide burden on soils and nearby water systems.

Textile dyeing is the second largest polluter of clean water globally. Recycling pre-dyed garments into coloured-sorted streams can dramatically reduce the need for re-dyeing.

The Circular Economy Case

The Ellen MacArthur Foundation estimates that moving to a circular model in fashion — where materials stay in use rather than going to waste — could generate $560 billion in economic value annually, while dramatically reducing fashion's environmental footprint.

Wornout recyclable fashion sits at the heart of this. It's the proof that the end of one garment's life is the beginning of another's material cycle. It closes the loop. And closing the loop is the only way fashion has any future worth wearing.
